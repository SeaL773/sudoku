Level 1 — Basics
Lesson 1

How Sudoku Works

Sudoku is a logic puzzle played on a 9x9 grid. The grid is divided into nine 3x3 boxes. Your goal is to fill every empty cell with a number from 1 to 9.

There are three rules, and they never change:

  • Every row must contain each digit 1-9 exactly once.
  • Every column must contain each digit 1-9 exactly once.
  • Every 3x3 box must contain each digit 1-9 exactly once.
Key insight: You never guess. Every cell has exactly one correct answer, and you can always find it through logic alone.

The puzzle starts with some cells already filled in — these are called givens. The more givens, the easier the puzzle. Your job is to deduce the rest.

Lesson 2

Last Free Cell

The simplest technique: if a row, column, or box already has 8 of the 9 digits filled in, the last empty cell must hold the one missing digit.

No elimination needed. Just count what's there and fill in what's missing.

Tip: Scan every row, column, and box at the start of a new puzzle. You'll often find several "last free cell" placements right away.

Lesson 3

Scanning (Last Remaining Cell)

Scanning means looking at where a digit already appears in the grid to figure out where it must go in a particular box.

Here's the logic: if a digit already exists in a row that passes through a box, that digit can't appear again in that row inside the box. Do the same for columns. If enough rows and columns are blocked, only one cell in the box remains available for that digit.

  • Pick a digit (say, 5).
  • Find all rows and columns that already contain a 5.
  • Cross out those rows and columns inside the target box.
  • If only one cell is left uncrossed, place the 5 there.
Where to start: Digits that appear most often in the grid (7 or 8 times) are the easiest to scan for, because most positions are already blocked.
Example — Scanning for 5

The yellow cells show where 5 already appears in crossing rows and columns. Only one cell in the center-left box (blue) is free. That's where 5 goes. Click "Show Answer" to confirm.

Level 2 — Notes & Singles
Lesson 4

Using Notes

When a cell can't be solved immediately, write down all the digits that could go there. These small numbers are called pencil marks or notes.

To find the candidates for a cell, look at its row, column, and box. Any digit already present in those three units is eliminated. Whatever's left is a valid candidate.

As you fill in more cells, go back and erase notes that are no longer valid. Over time, cells with many candidates narrow down to just one or two, making them solvable.

When to start using notes: Easy puzzles can often be solved without them. Once you're stuck and can't find any naked or hidden singles by eye, it's time to write notes for every empty cell.

Lesson 5

Naked Singles

A naked single is a cell where only one candidate remains after all other digits have been eliminated by its peers.

Once you've written notes for every empty cell, look for any cell with exactly one note. That note is the answer. Place it, then update the notes of all cells in the same row, column, and box.

Placing one naked single often creates another, triggering a chain reaction that solves large portions of the puzzle.

Workflow: Fill notes for all cells, then sweep the board for naked singles. Repeat until no more naked singles exist, then move to hidden singles.

Lesson 6

Hidden Singles

A hidden single is a digit that can only go in one cell within a row, column, or box, even though that cell may have multiple candidates.

The digit is "hidden" because the cell looks like it has options. But when you check the rest of the unit, every other cell is blocked from holding that digit. So it must go here.

This is the most important technique for intermediate puzzles. Many puzzles that seem stuck become solvable once you start looking for hidden singles.

  • Pick a unit (row, column, or box).
  • Pick a digit that isn't placed yet in that unit.
  • Check which cells in the unit can hold that digit.
  • If only one cell can hold it, place it there.
Hidden vs. Naked: Naked singles are found by looking at a cell and seeing one candidate. Hidden singles are found by looking at a unit and seeing one possible position for a digit.

Level 3 — Pairs & Beyond
Lesson 7

Naked Pairs

A naked pair occurs when two cells in the same unit each have exactly the same two candidates, and no others.

If two cells in a row both have only candidates [4, 7], then 4 and 7 must go in those two cells (in some order). That means no other cell in that row can contain 4 or 7. You can safely remove 4 and 7 from the notes of every other cell in the unit.

This doesn't tell you which cell gets 4 and which gets 7, but it clears candidates from other cells, which can unlock naked or hidden singles elsewhere.

Why it works: Two cells, two digits. Those two digits are "used up" by those two cells. The rest of the unit can't have them.
Example — Naked Pair [4, 7] in Row 4

Cells at columns 2 and 6 in row 4 both have only candidates [4, 7] (blue). The yellow cells in the same row had 4 or 7 in their notes. Those candidates can now be removed.

Lesson 8

Pointing Pairs

A pointing pair (or pointing triple) occurs when all the possible positions for a candidate within a box fall in the same row or column.

If digit 6 can only go in two cells within a box, and both those cells are in row 3, then 6 must end up in row 3 somewhere in that box. That means 6 can't appear anywhere else in row 3 outside the box. You can eliminate 6 from the notes of all other cells in that row.

The same logic applies to columns: if the candidates for a digit in a box all fall in one column, eliminate that digit from the rest of the column.

Direction matters: The elimination goes outward from the box into the row or column, not the other way around.
Example — Pointing Pair for digit 6

In the top-left box, digit 6 can only go in the two blue cells, both in row 1. So 6 can be removed from the yellow cells in row 1 outside the box.

Lesson 9

Naked Triples

A naked triple extends the naked pair concept to three cells. Three cells in the same unit collectively contain only three candidates (or subsets of those three candidates). Those three digits are "used up" by those three cells and can be eliminated from all other cells in the unit.

The cells don't all need to have all three candidates. The rule is that the union of candidates across the three cells contains exactly three digits. For example:

  • Cell A: [1, 3, 7]
  • Cell B: [1, 7]
  • Cell C: [3, 7]

The union is {1, 3, 7} — three digits in three cells. Remove 1, 3, and 7 from every other cell in that unit.

Spotting them: Look for three cells in a unit whose combined candidates total exactly three digits. Cells with only two candidates are common members of naked triples.
Example — Naked Triple [1, 3, 7] in Row 6

Three blue cells in row 6 hold only candidates from {1, 3, 7}. The yellow cell previously had 1 and 7 in its notes — both can now be removed, leaving it a naked single.

Lesson 10

Hidden Pairs

A hidden pair is the complement of a naked pair. Two digits appear as candidates in only two cells within a unit. Those two cells must contain those two digits — so all other candidates in those two cells can be removed.

The pair is "hidden" because the two cells appear to have many candidates. But once you notice that two specific digits only appear in those two cells within the unit, you know those cells are locked to those two digits.

  • Scan a unit for a digit that appears in only two cells.
  • Check if another digit also appears in only those same two cells.
  • If so, you have a hidden pair. Remove all other candidates from those two cells.
Hidden vs. Naked: A naked pair has exactly two candidates per cell. A hidden pair has extra candidates that get eliminated once you spot the pair. The end result is the same — two cells locked to two digits.
Example — Hidden Pair {2, 8} in a Box

In the center box, digits 2 and 8 only appear as candidates in the two blue cells. Those cells also have other candidates (shown in notes). All candidates except 2 and 8 can be removed from those two cells.

Lesson 11

Hidden Triples

A hidden triple extends hidden pairs to three digits and three cells. Three digits appear as candidates in only three cells within a unit. Those three cells must contain those three digits, so all other candidates in those three cells can be eliminated.

Hidden triples are harder to spot than hidden pairs because the three cells each have multiple candidates, and the three "hidden" digits may not all appear in every one of the three cells — they just collectively appear nowhere else in the unit.

  • Find three digits in a unit that only appear in three cells (combined).
  • Those three cells are locked to those three digits.
  • Remove all other candidates from those three cells.
Practical tip: Hidden triples are rare and difficult to find manually. If you're solving by hand, focus on naked triples first — they're easier to spot. Hidden triples become more relevant when using systematic candidate analysis.
Example — Hidden Triple {2, 5, 9} in Column 3

In column 3, digits 2, 5, and 9 only appear as candidates in the three blue cells. Other candidates in those cells (shown in notes) can all be removed, potentially creating naked singles or pairs.

Lesson 12

Pointing Triples

A pointing triple works exactly like a pointing pair (Lesson 8), but with three cells instead of two. If a candidate in a box appears in only three cells, and all three cells fall in the same row or column, that candidate can be eliminated from the rest of that row or column outside the box.

The logic is identical: those three cells in the box "claim" that row or column for that digit. No other cell in that row or column can hold it.

Pointing pairs vs. triples: Both work the same way. The only difference is whether two or three cells in the box are aligned. In practice, pointing pairs are more common, but triples appear regularly in medium and hard puzzles.

In the center-right box, digit 7 can only go in three cells (blue), all in column 5. So 7 can be removed from the yellow cells in column 5 outside the box.

Level 4 — Advanced
Lesson 13

X-Wing

The X-Wing is the first technique that looks across multiple rows and columns simultaneously. It targets a candidate that appears in exactly two cells in each of two different rows, where those cells align in the same two columns.

Here's the logic: if digit 4 appears in only two cells in row 2 (columns 3 and 8), and also in only two cells in row 7 (the same columns 3 and 8), then digit 4 must go in one of those four cells. Specifically, it must occupy column 3 in one row and column 8 in the other. Either way, columns 3 and 8 each get exactly one 4 from this pattern. So 4 can be eliminated from all other cells in columns 3 and 8.

The four cells form a rectangle — the "X" shape that gives the technique its name.

  • Find a candidate that appears in exactly 2 cells in row A and exactly 2 cells in row B.
  • Check if those cells share the same two columns.
  • If yes: eliminate that candidate from all other cells in those two columns.
  • The same pattern works with columns instead of rows (eliminate from rows).
Why it works: The four cells form two possible solutions (diagonal pairs). In both cases, each of the two columns gets exactly one instance of the digit. So no other cell in those columns can hold it.
Example — X-Wing on digit 4 in rows 2 and 7

Digit 4 appears in only two cells in row 2 (blue) and only two cells in row 7 (blue), both pairs in columns 3 and 8. The yellow cells in columns 3 and 8 can have 4 eliminated from their notes.

Lesson 14

Y-Wing (XY-Wing)

The Y-Wing (also called XY-Wing) is a three-cell chain technique. It involves a pivot cell and two wing cells, connected by shared candidates.

The setup:

  • Pivot cell: has exactly two candidates [A, B].
  • Wing 1: shares a unit with the pivot, has candidates [A, C].
  • Wing 2: shares a unit with the pivot, has candidates [B, C].

The logic: the pivot must be either A or B. If it's A, then Wing 1 (which shares A with the pivot) must be C. If it's B, then Wing 2 (which shares B with the pivot) must be C. Either way, at least one wing is C. Any cell that sees both wings cannot be C — eliminate C from those cells.

Key requirement: The two wings don't need to share a unit with each other — they only need to each share a unit with the pivot. The elimination targets cells that see both wings simultaneously.
Example — Y-Wing with pivot [3, 7], wings [3, 5] and [7, 5]

The blue pivot cell has candidates [3, 7]. Wing 1 (green) shares its row and has [3, 5]. Wing 2 (green) shares its column and has [7, 5]. The yellow cell sees both wings — candidate 5 can be eliminated from it.

Lesson 15

Swordfish

The Swordfish is the three-row (or three-column) extension of the X-Wing. Instead of two rows and two columns, it uses three rows and three columns.

A candidate forms a Swordfish when it appears in 2 or 3 cells in each of three rows, and all those cells fall within the same three columns. In that case, the candidate can be eliminated from all other cells in those three columns.

The logic mirrors X-Wing: across the three rows, the candidate must be placed in the three columns exactly once each. So no other cell in those columns can hold it.

  • Find a candidate that appears in 2-3 cells in row A, row B, and row C.
  • Check that all those cells fall within the same three columns.
  • Eliminate that candidate from all other cells in those three columns.
  • The same pattern works column-first (eliminate from rows).
Swordfish vs. X-Wing: X-Wing is a 2x2 rectangle. Swordfish is a 3x3 pattern. Both follow the same logic — the candidate is "locked" into a set of rows/columns and can be eliminated from the rest. Jellyfish (4x4) follows the same principle further.
Example — Swordfish on digit 2 across rows 1, 4, and 7

Digit 2 appears in only 2-3 cells in rows 1, 4, and 7 (blue), all within columns 2, 5, and 8. The yellow cells in those three columns can have 2 eliminated from their notes.

Where to go from here: You've covered the full standard technique set. Beyond Swordfish, puzzles may require Jellyfish, Skyscraper, Two-String Kite, or forcing chains. These are rare in published puzzles but appear in "extreme" difficulty levels. The best way to learn them is to keep playing and look up each technique as you encounter it.
