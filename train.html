Level 1 — Basics
Lesson 1

How Sudoku Works

Sudoku is a logic puzzle played on a 9x9 grid. The grid is divided into nine 3x3 boxes. Your goal is to fill every empty cell with a number from 1 to 9.

There are three rules, and they never change:

  • Every row must contain each digit 1-9 exactly once.
  • Every column must contain each digit 1-9 exactly once.
  • Every 3x3 box must contain each digit 1-9 exactly once.
Key insight: You never guess. Every cell has exactly one correct answer, and you can always find it through logic alone.

The puzzle starts with some cells already filled in — these are called givens. The more givens, the easier the puzzle. Your job is to deduce the rest.

Lesson 2

Last Free Cell

The simplest technique: if a row, column, or box already has 8 of the 9 digits filled in, the last empty cell must hold the one missing digit.

No elimination needed. Just count what's there and fill in what's missing.

Tip: Scan every row, column, and box at the start of a new puzzle. You'll often find several "last free cell" placements right away.

Lesson 3

Scanning (Last Remaining Cell)

Scanning means looking at where a digit already appears in the grid to figure out where it must go in a particular box.

Here's the logic: if a digit already exists in a row that passes through a box, that digit can't appear again in that row inside the box. Do the same for columns. If enough rows and columns are blocked, only one cell in the box remains available for that digit.

  • Pick a digit (say, 5).
  • Find all rows and columns that already contain a 5.
  • Cross out those rows and columns inside the target box.
  • If only one cell is left uncrossed, place the 5 there.
Where to start: Digits that appear most often in the grid (7 or 8 times) are the easiest to scan for, because most positions are already blocked.
Example — Scanning for 5

The yellow cells show where 5 already appears in crossing rows and columns. Only one cell in the center-left box (blue) is free. That's where 5 goes. Click "Show Answer" to confirm.

Level 2 — Notes & Singles
Lesson 4

Using Notes

When a cell can't be solved immediately, write down all the digits that could go there. These small numbers are called pencil marks or notes.

To find the candidates for a cell, look at its row, column, and box. Any digit already present in those three units is eliminated. Whatever's left is a valid candidate.

As you fill in more cells, go back and erase notes that are no longer valid. Over time, cells with many candidates narrow down to just one or two, making them solvable.

When to start using notes: Easy puzzles can often be solved without them. Once you're stuck and can't find any naked or hidden singles by eye, it's time to write notes for every empty cell.

Lesson 5

Naked Singles

A naked single is a cell where only one candidate remains after all other digits have been eliminated by its peers.

Once you've written notes for every empty cell, look for any cell with exactly one note. That note is the answer. Place it, then update the notes of all cells in the same row, column, and box.

Placing one naked single often creates another, triggering a chain reaction that solves large portions of the puzzle.

Workflow: Fill notes for all cells, then sweep the board for naked singles. Repeat until no more naked singles exist, then move to hidden singles.

Lesson 6

Hidden Singles

A hidden single is a digit that can only go in one cell within a row, column, or box, even though that cell may have multiple candidates.

The digit is "hidden" because the cell looks like it has options. But when you check the rest of the unit, every other cell is blocked from holding that digit. So it must go here.

This is the most important technique for intermediate puzzles. Many puzzles that seem stuck become solvable once you start looking for hidden singles.

  • Pick a unit (row, column, or box).
  • Pick a digit that isn't placed yet in that unit.
  • Check which cells in the unit can hold that digit.
  • If only one cell can hold it, place it there.
Hidden vs. Naked: Naked singles are found by looking at a cell and seeing one candidate. Hidden singles are found by looking at a unit and seeing one possible position for a digit.

Level 3 — Pairs & Beyond
Lesson 7

Naked Pairs

A naked pair occurs when two cells in the same unit each have exactly the same two candidates, and no others.

If two cells in a row both have only candidates [4, 7], then 4 and 7 must go in those two cells (in some order). That means no other cell in that row can contain 4 or 7. You can safely remove 4 and 7 from the notes of every other cell in the unit.

This doesn't tell you which cell gets 4 and which gets 7, but it clears candidates from other cells, which can unlock naked or hidden singles elsewhere.

Why it works: Two cells, two digits. Those two digits are "used up" by those two cells. The rest of the unit can't have them.
Example — Naked Pair [4, 7] in Row 4

Cells at columns 2 and 6 in row 4 both have only candidates [4, 7] (blue). The yellow cells in the same row had 4 or 7 in their notes. Those candidates can now be removed.

Lesson 8

Pointing Pairs

A pointing pair (or pointing triple) occurs when all the possible positions for a candidate within a box fall in the same row or column.

If digit 6 can only go in two cells within a box, and both those cells are in row 3, then 6 must end up in row 3 somewhere in that box. That means 6 can't appear anywhere else in row 3 outside the box. You can eliminate 6 from the notes of all other cells in that row.

The same logic applies to columns: if the candidates for a digit in a box all fall in one column, eliminate that digit from the rest of the column.

Direction matters: The elimination goes outward from the box into the row or column, not the other way around.
Example — Pointing Pair for digit 6

In the top-left box, digit 6 can only go in the two blue cells, both in row 1. So 6 can be removed from the yellow cells in row 1 outside the box.

What's next? After naked pairs and pointing pairs, the next techniques are hidden pairs, X-Wings, and Swordfish. These follow the same logical principles but look at larger patterns. The best way to learn them is to keep playing and look up each technique as you encounter it.
